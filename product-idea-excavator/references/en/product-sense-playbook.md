# Product Sense Playbook

## Core Principle

Product discovery is not about organizing what the user says. It is about turning vague desire into testable product judgment.

Priority order:

1. real demand evidence
2. specific user and status-quo cost
3. narrowest wedge
4. explicit non-goals
5. technical and operational feasibility
6. observable success metrics

## Demand Evidence

Interest is not demand.

- Weak signal: "sounds interesting", waitlist signup, friendly encouragement.
- Medium signal: user shares real data, schedules another call, shows the current workflow.
- Strong signal: payment, prepayment, repeated usage, workflow replacement, urgent complaints when the process fails.

If the user only has weak signals, suggest a validation task before expanding scope.

## Current Alternative

The status quo is often the real competitor.

Ask:

- What do users do today?
- Which tools are stitched together?
- Who handles it manually?
- How many hours or dollars does it cost?
- Why have they not switched already?

If the user says "there is no solution", be careful. It may be opportunity, or it may mean the pain is not strong enough.

## Specific User

"SMBs", "creators", and "product managers" are not specific enough.

Push toward:

- role
- context
- task
- pressure
- budget
- success criteria
- failure consequence

## Narrowest Wedge

Early products should not chase platform completeness.

Ask:

- What is the smallest result someone would use or pay for this week?
- Can we deliver the result before building the full system?
- Can manual or semi-automated delivery validate the idea?
- Which wedge creates retention, payment, or strong feedback fastest?

## MVP Cutting

MVP is not the fewest features. It is the smallest version that validates value.

Sequence:

1. What is the user's most painful moment?
2. What action proves they need this?
3. Which features are required for that action?
4. Which features merely make the experience more complete?
5. Which features slow validation?

## Premise Challenge

Before locking the solution, challenge:

- Is this the right problem?
- Will users change behavior?
- Is the solution taking a detour?
- Is the technology prematurely complex?
- Are metrics becoming proxies?
- Can the validation be smaller?

## Alternatives

Do not recommend only one path.

Consider:

- minimal validation path
- robust MVP path
- long-term architecture path
- counterintuitive wedge

For each, explain cost, risk, when it fits, and your recommendation.

## Retention

Ask where retention comes from:

- data accumulation
- team collaboration
- repeated workflow
- content updates
- business results
- network effects

No retention source usually means the product risks becoming a one-time tool.

## Observing Users

Interviews are not observation. What users say and what they do often differ.

Recommend watching users complete the current workflow without guiding them. Capture friction, copy-paste behavior, spreadsheets, chat coordination, and the user's own language.

## Future Fit

Ask:

- Does future technology make the product more necessary or easier to replace?
- Will workflows centralize or fragment?
- Will data become more open or more locked down?
- Does the moat come from model, data, workflow, distribution, or trust?

## Quality Gate

Before PRD synthesis, answer or mark as open:

- Who feels the pain most?
- What do they do today?
- What does it cost?
- What evidence proves demand?
- What is the narrowest wedge?
- Why is this better than the status quo?
- What metric proves value?
- What happens if the riskiest assumption is wrong?
